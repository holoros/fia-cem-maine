# Maine Forest Carbon Through 2074: Scenario Projections under Climate, Market, and Landowner Behavior

**Prepared for:** Maine Climate Council, Forestry Working Group

**Prepared by:** A. Weiskittel, Center for Research on Sustainable Forests, University of Maine

**Date:** April 2026 (refresh, refined-pipeline tag r18)

---

## Bottom line

Maine's projected forest above-ground live tree carbon is highly sensitive to harvest intensity, with landowner-class behavior emerging as a first-order driver alongside it. Under business-as-usual harvest (2 percent of area per year, 50 percent biomass removal) and moderate warming (RCP 4.5), projected AGC drops from 237 MMT in 2004 to 44 MMT by 2074, an 81 percent decline. Under a no-harvest counterfactual, AGC stabilizes at 97 MMT by 2074. The five harvest-intensity scenarios produce a 67 MMT spread by 2074 under the refined pipeline (Maine econ overlay, RCP 4.5):

| Scenario | 2074 AGC | Annual loss |
|---|---:|---:|
| No_harvest | 97 MMT | −2.1 MMT/yr |
| Half BAU | 65 MMT | −2.5 MMT/yr |
| BAU | 44 MMT | −2.8 MMT/yr |
| +25 percent (pulp demand) | 36 MMT | −2.9 MMT/yr |
| +50 percent (biomass expansion) | 30 MMT | −2.9 MMT/yr |

A 50 percent increase in harvest intensity reduces 2074 AGC by 14 MMT relative to BAU; halving the harvest rate preserves 21 MMT relative to BAU. The relationship is approximately linear in harvest intensity over the tested range. Climate scenario differences (RCP 4.5 vs 8.5) are small under harvest (about 5 to 10 MMT) and modest under no-harvest (22 MMT, with RCP 8.5 ending higher because CO2 fertilization outweighs species damage at Maine's mid-latitude position).

**New finding (this refresh):** Stratifying harvest probability by ownership class using the Harris-Caputo-Butler (2025) ownership raster substantially improves projection skill. The forest-area-weighted mean of owner-class harvest multipliers (NIPF 0.5, Industrial 1.5, Tribal/Federal 0.2, State 0.5, Local 0.3) is 0.81, reflecting that Maine's NIPF-dominant southern counties harvest at lower rates than the industrial north. Recognizing this asymmetry reduces hindcast RMSE from 22 MMT (uniform-rate) to 14.5 MMT (owner-stratified) and brings hindcast bias from −20 MMT down to −6 MMT.

These projections integrate 10 years of Maine Forest Service county stumpage data, 9 years of Silvicultural Activities Reports, FIA plot-level inventory through 2024, the Harris-Caputo-Butler 2025 ownership raster, the Potter et al. 2017 species climate vulnerability database, and a Bayesian Reineke maximum-density posterior fit to 173,000 eastern US plots.

## Why this matters for the Climate Council

The Maine Climate Action Plan targets a 45 percent reduction in greenhouse gas emissions from 1990 levels by 2030 and carbon neutrality by 2045. Forest carbon is a central sink in that accounting. Reliable projections at the state scale help the Council calibrate three policy levers:

1. **Forest management incentives.** Tree Growth Tax Law, Keeping Maine's Forests Forest, and other programs affect harvest intensity and thus the harvest-vs-growth balance.

2. **Market signals.** Carbon offset pricing and wood-product market conditions shift which plots get cut when. The stumpage-price-sensitivity results show that a ±50 percent price swing changes carbon stocks by ±15 to 20 MMT over the next 25 years.

3. **Climate adaptation.** Species composition, disturbance risk, and growth-rate response all evolve with warming. The difference between RCP 4.5 and RCP 8.5 is modest in the near term (less than 5 MMT by 2050) but substantial by 2099 (approximately 20 MMT) under harvest.

## Summary of findings

### 1. Forest carbon declines under all harvest scenarios

Above-ground live tree carbon drops from 237 MMT in 2004 to 19 to 65 MMT by 2074 across the harvest scenarios (RCP 4.5 wear+econ). Total carbon (including soil, dead wood, and understory pools) drops from 1,131 MMT to 232 to 332 MMT in the same window. The magnitude of decline is steeper than earlier (uniform-rate) projections because the refined pipeline includes a Reineke maximum-stand-density cap, an episodic disturbance module (spruce budworm, wind, fire), and species-specific climate vulnerability that throttles spruce-fir growth under warming. These mechanisms collectively prevent the unbounded biomass accumulation that earlier coarsened-exact-matching pipelines produced under RCP 8.5 no-harvest.

### 2. No-harvest scenarios stabilize carbon

Under the no-harvest counterfactual, above-ground live carbon trajects from 247 MMT in 2004 to 97 MMT by 2074 (RCP 4.5) or 119 MMT (RCP 8.5). The 22 MMT positive divergence under RCP 8.5 is driven by CO2 fertilization (Norby et al. 2010) outweighing species-vulnerability damage at Maine's latitude. The preserved no-harvest carbon stock is substantial: avoided emissions of approximately 70 MMT (relative to BAU) over the projection horizon, equivalent to 257 MMT CO2-equivalent at the standard biomass-to-CO2 conversion.

### 3. The partial-versus-clearcut split matters and is owner-driven

Maine's harvest pattern is overwhelmingly partial (93 percent of harvested area versus 7 percent clearcut, 2015-2023 SAR average), and the partial-clearcut split is strongly correlated with landowner class. Aroostook 26 percent clearcut share (industrial-dominated, 47 percent of forest in HCB Class 4), Piscataquis 21 percent (industrial 44 percent), Somerset 21 percent (industrial 36 percent). Southern coastal counties show under 1 percent clearcut share with NIPF dominance over 80 percent. The refined pipeline's owner-stratified harvest multipliers (NIPF 0.5, Industrial 1.5) capture this directly; the resulting forest-area-weighted mean rate (0.81 of uniform) reflects that Maine's NIPF dominance softens statewide harvest below the area-uniform expectation.

### 4. Landowner stratification is the largest single calibration improvement

Adding Harris-Caputo-Butler (2025) ownership-class harvest behavior to the projection cuts hindcast RMSE from 22 MMT (uniform-rate, r17) to 14.5 MMT (owner-stratified, r18) — a 35 percent reduction. Hindcast bias improves from −20 MMT to −6 MMT against subject-matched observed FIA inventory at 5-year intervals from 2004 to 2024. Per-year residuals shift from r17's monotonic undershoot pattern (−32, −23, −8, −9, −28 MMT) to r18's tighter tracking (−27, −12, +7, +10, −7 MMT). The owner-class refinement is more consequential to projection accuracy than the SDImax cap, climate decoupling, or species vulnerability layers individually.

### 5. Climate warming effect is muted by harvest dominance

Under RCP 8.5 warming (HadGEM2-AO, MACAv2 downscaled), projected growth response is modest. Decoupled CO2 fertilization adds approximately 3 percent productivity per 1 C warming (saturating above 3 C), while species vulnerability (Potter 2017 CAPTURE framework) imposes a 9 percent growth penalty per 4.5 C on red and black spruce. The two effects partially offset, leaving climate as a small modifier compared with harvest decisions. The compressed harvest range under the refined pipeline (BAU to +50 percent biomass: 14 MMT spread by 2074) reflects that the SDImax cap and disturbance module trim the carbon ceiling, so high-intensity harvest scenarios converge toward similar low endpoints.

## Policy implications

### Short-term (2025-2030)

Carbon stock changes under any of the scenarios modeled are small (less than 5 MMT per year). Forest management decisions made in this window will predominantly affect the 2040s and 2050s carbon trajectory rather than 2030 targets. State-scale carbon accounting should rely on observed FIA inventories rather than projections for near-term verification.

### Medium-term (2030-2074)

By 2074, the five harvest scenarios bracket a 67 MMT range in projected AGC under the refined pipeline (RCP 4.5 wear+econ). Key leverage points:

- Halving the harvest rate preserves 21 MMT additional AGC by 2074 (44 to 65 MMT).
- A 25 percent increase in harvest intensity reduces 2074 AGC by 8 MMT relative to BAU (44 to 36 MMT).
- A 50 percent increase reduces 2074 AGC by 14 MMT relative to BAU (44 to 30 MMT).
- The relationship is approximately linear over the tested range. The compressed spread (compared with earlier reports) reflects the SDImax cap and disturbance module imposing a lower carbon ceiling that high-harvest scenarios converge toward.

**Owner-class effect.** Differential management by ownership type produces a 13 to 17 MMT shift in 2074 AGC compared with uniform-rate projections. NIPF-dominated areas retain more carbon than uniform-rate models predict; industrial-dominated areas lose more. Policy levers that target owner classes (BPL light-management practice, conservation easement programs, Farm Bill payments to NIPF, mill-supply contracts with industrial owners) act differently and should be evaluated against owner-stratified rather than uniform projections.

These deltas hold across both RCP 4.5 and 8.5, indicating that harvest decisions dominate climate forcing for Maine carbon outcomes through 2074.

### Long-term (2074-2100)

Projections beyond 2074 are not reported in this brief because the current pipeline runs to 2074 (15 cycles from 1999 baseline). Extending to 2099 (20 cycles) would require an additional 4 hours of compute per scenario set. Periodic re-runs (every 5 years) as new FIA panels come available would update the projections with the latest observed trajectories.

## Technical notes for the Forestry Working Group

### What the projections are based on

- FIA measurement panels 1999 to 2024 (22 years of observed data)
- MFS Stumpage Price Reports 2015 to 2024 (10 annual reports, county-level)
- MFS Silvicultural Activities Reports 2015 to 2023 (9 annual reports)
- HadGEM2-AO MACAv2 downscaled climate projections for RCP 4.5 and 8.5
- Wear and Coulston (2019, 2025) forest sector model coefficients

### What they are NOT

- They are not land use change projections. Forest-to-development conversion is held at the 2023 SAR rate of 5,295 acres per year (2 percent of total harvest) as a constant.
- They are not disturbance projections. Severe disturbance (insect outbreaks, windstorms, wildfire) is implicitly captured through the FIA donor pool but not scaled up under climate warming.
- They are not market-responsive in the harvest-timing sense. Plots are allocated to harvest by an economic logit but the total harvested area per year is pinned to the 2015 to 2023 SAR average.

### Uncertainty

Projected carbon stocks carry a 95 percent credible interval of approximately ±15 percent at 2050 and ±25 percent at 2099. This interval reflects plot-level uncertainty from bootstrap resampling but does not reflect structural uncertainty in the climate or economic submodels, nor scenario uncertainty across RCPs. A conservative policy assessment should treat the 2050 carbon estimate as 530 to 620 MMT total (with 595 MMT as the central estimate).

### Cross-validation

A 1999 to 2024 hindcast was run for both the uniform-rate (r17) and owner-stratified (r18) refined pipelines, validated against subject-matched observed FIA inventory at 5-year intervals (2004, 2009, 2014, 2019, 2024).

| Pipeline | RMSE (MMT) | Bias (MMT) | MAE (MMT) |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Uniform-rate refined (r17) RCP 4.5 wear | 22.4 | −20.1 | 20.1 |
| Uniform-rate refined (r17) RCP 8.5 wear | 18.7 | −15.1 | 15.1 |
| Owner-stratified (r18) RCP 4.5 wear | **14.5** | **−5.8** | 12.5 |
| Owner-stratified (r18) RCP 8.5 wear | 15.4 | **−0.1** | 13.3 |

The owner-stratified pipeline (r18) is the recommended canonical version for state-scale carbon projections. The 14.5 MMT RMSE corresponds to about 6 percent of the mean stock — appropriate predictive skill for state-scale planning. Compared with full-panel EXPALL inventory, an apparent gap of approximately 90 MMT remains; this is the definitional mismatch between projection scope (subject plots only) and full inventory scope, not model error.

## Recommendations

1. **For MCC GHG accounting:** Use observed FIA panels for near-term (2025-2030) carbon stock verification, reserving CEM projections (r18 owner-stratified) for medium- to long-term scenario planning.

2. **For forest policy:** Market-based levers (stumpage price changes) have asymmetric effects on harvest timing and carbon stocks. Area-based and intensity-based management levers are roughly linearly effective. Owner-class policy levers (BPL practice, conservation easements, Farm Bill payments to NIPF, mill contracts with industrials) deserve dedicated treatment because owner behavior is a first-order determinant of state-scale carbon outcome that uniform-rate models miss.

3. **For monitoring and evaluation:** The HCB ownership raster is updated approximately every 5 years. Re-running the projection on a 5-year cadence as new FIA panels and HCB updates land would keep state-scale carbon estimates current. Maine-specific updates of the ownership raster (e.g., capturing recent TIMO sales and conservation acquisitions like the Pingree-Crown easement) would tighten the predictive skill further.

4. **For cross-model coherence with FVS, GCBM, and LANDIS:** The HCB classification is recommended as the lingua franca for owner stratification across the four-model PERSEUS ensemble. A shared owner-class harvest behavioral parameter set (calibrated to Maine SAR by-owner) would eliminate one source of cross-model disagreement currently absorbed into structural uncertainty bands.

3. **For future re-analysis:** Re-run the pipeline every 5 years as new FIA panels come available. The annualized cycle produces a new EVALID every year; a 5-year re-run captures a full panel cycle of new data.

4. **For cross-state comparison:** The same pipeline can be applied to any state with comparable FIA and stumpage reporting infrastructure (e.g., New Hampshire, Vermont, Michigan, Wisconsin). Cross-state comparison would strengthen the case for regional policy coordination.
